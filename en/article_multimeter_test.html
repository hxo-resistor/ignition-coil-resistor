How to Test an Ignition Coil Suppressor Resistor with a Multimeter

HXO Resistor · Practical Repair Guide · July 2026

When you encounter engine misfire, unstable idle, or loss of power, the ignition system is often the culprit. The suppressor resistor is one of the most common failure points in an ignition coil — if it's bad, replacing just the resistor (costing a few dollars) can fix the problem without replacing the entire coil assembly.

Here's how to diagnose a suppressor resistor in 3 minutes with a digital multimeter.

Preparation

  • Tool: Digital multimeter (DT830 or above, basic models work fine)
  • Safety: Ensure the vehicle is off, key removed, wait 5 minutes for capacitor discharge
  • Location: The suppressor resistor is typically inside the spark plug cap or at the ignition coil secondary output

Measurement Steps

1Set multimeter to resistance (Ω), select 20kΩ range

If using auto-ranging, just select Ω. For manual ranging, secondary suppressor resistors are typically 4.7kΩ~10kΩ, so 20kΩ is the most suitable range.

2Measure across the resistor

Touch the red and black probes to the two resistor leads. Ensure good contact — don't measure through insulating paint.

3Read the value and diagnose

Refer to the table below:

Measurement ResultDiagnosisCauseAction
4.5kΩ~10.5kΩNormalWithin specified toleranceNo replacement needed
20%+ above nominalAgingLong-term high temp causing driftReplace recommended
Infinite (OL/1)Open circuitInternal burnout or lead fractureMust replace — misfire will occur
Near 0ΩShort circuitInsulation breakdown or lead bridgingMust replace — severe EMI
Unstable readingPoor contactInternal solder joint crackReplace recommended

⚠️ Note: Primary suppressor resistors (0.5Ω~1.5Ω) require the 200Ω or lower range. Using the 20kΩ range will show 0Ω, incorrectly diagnosing a short circuit.

4 Common Abnormal Conditions

1. Increased Resistance (Aging)

Most common issue. Long-term operation at 100°C~200°C causes the resistive material to slowly oxidize, increasing resistance. Initially only a slight drop in ignition energy, but eventually causes misfire.

2. Open Circuit (Burnout)

Typically caused by instantaneous overvoltage — excessive spark plug gap or leaking high-voltage wires subjecting the resistor to voltage exceeding its rated pulse withstand.

3. Short Circuit (Near 0Ω)

Uncommon but serious. The resistor loses its EMI suppression function, allowing high-frequency oscillations to radiate and interfere with ECUs, ABS, and other onboard electronics.

4. Unstable Reading

Tapping the resistor body causes the reading to fluctuate, indicating internal cracks or loose solder joints. This resistor is at risk of failure in vibration environments.

📌 Quick Reference: Secondary resistance in kΩ range, 4.7~10 is normal. Infinite means open circuit, near 0Ω means short. More than 20% above nominal indicates aging. Fluctuating readings indicate internal damage.
